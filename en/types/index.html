Types of Misinformation

To combat fake news effectively, it is essential to understand that misinformation does not present itself in a single way. It manifests through different categories, each with varying levels of intent and potential for harm. Over the years, communication experts have identified patterns that help us classify these contents and understand how they attempt to manipulate public perception.

Completely Fabricated Content

This is the purest form of fake news, where the information is invented from scratch without any basis in reality. Generally, these contents are created with the explicit goal of deceiving the public for financial gain through clicks or to defame public figures. They often use names of fictional institutions or visually imitate renowned news sites to gain an immediate appearance of credibility.

Manipulated and Edited Content

Manipulation occurs when real information, such as photos, videos, or audios, is digitally altered to change its original meaning. With the advancement of editing software, it has become common to find retouched images to create situations that never happened or videos edited to change the meaning of a statement. The goal here is to distort visual evidence, which is historically seen as irrefutable proof of truth.

Out-of-Context Content

Often, misinformation uses facts that are technically true but presents them out of their original context. This happens, for example, when a news story from five years ago is shared as if it occurred today, or when a real graph is used to support a conclusion that is unrelated to the data presented. This technique is particularly dangerous because, by containing elements of truth, it becomes much harder to debunk.

In addition to these main categories, we also find satire or parody, which although not initially intended to deceive, can be interpreted as truth by inattentive readers when taken out of its humorous environment. Understanding these nuances is the first step toward developing a critical eye for everything we consume in the digital environment, ensuring that the information we share is always based on solid and verifiable facts.
